1. Game Concept

Hot Potato is a single-NFT perpetual auction game deployed on Base. One NFT — The Hot Potato — is always for sale. Whoever holds it cannot refuse a sale. When bought, the previous holder automatically receives their ETH proceeds plus a freshly minted Souvenir NFT.

The game has two intertwined economies: the primary game (the potato itself, always escalating in price) and the souvenir economy (collectible NFTs whose rarity is earned by how you played).

Core insight: The potato is a hot seat, not an investment. You can't time your exit — someone buys you out. The psychological tension between "hold longer for a better souvenir" and "I might get stuck holding forever" is the game's engine.

2. Complete Rules

🔥 Rule 1 — The Potato is Always For Sale

The Hot Potato NFT (Token ID 0) is always listed for sale. The current owner cannot delist, refuse, or block a purchase. Any wallet can call buyPotato(newAskingPrice) at any time as long as they pay the current asking price.

📈 Rule 2 — Price Only Goes Up (Tiered Minimum)

Every buyer must set a new asking price above what they paid, enforced on-chain. The minimum increase scales down as the price rises to keep the game accessible at high valuations:

💸 Rule 3 — Fee Split on Every Sale

Every purchase is automatically split at the smart contract level: 95% goes to the previous holder (automatic, instant), 5% goes to the creator wallet. This is enforced on-chain and cannot be bypassed. The creator also earns ERC-2981 royalties on secondary souvenir sales on compliant marketplaces (e.g. OpenSea).

🎁 Rule 4 — Souvenir Minted on Every Sale

When the potato changes hands, a new Souvenir NFT is minted to the outgoing holder. The souvenir's rarity is determined by a pseudo-random roll weighted by hold duration and any active boosts. The souvenir is the primary collectible and the game's long-term social proof layer.

⏱ Rule 5 — Hold Longer, Get Rarer (Updated Tiers)

The longer you hold before being bought out, the better your rarity odds. Tiers are now compressed to match crypto-native attention horizons:

Buyers who pay significantly above the current asking price earn a premium boost stored on-chain, applied when they eventually get bought out. This creates a trade-off: paying more gives better souvenir odds but also raises your new asking floor.

🔥 Rule 7 — Trade-In: Burn a Souvenir for a Boost

Holders can burn (send to dead address) any souvenir they own to receive a single-use trade-in code. The boost scales with the rarity of the burned souvenir. This code is applied before buying and counts toward the next souvenir's rarity roll.

🏆 Rule 8 — Loyalty Boost (Claim-Based)

Wallets that have previously held the potato accumulate loyalty holds. Before buying, they can actively claim their loyalty boost — this stores a pending boost and resets the counter. If not claimed, the boost is not applied. After claiming and being bought out, the player must earn new holds to claim again.

🤝 Rule 9 — Referral System

Every wallet has a deterministic referral code (REF-XXXXXXXX). When a new buyer applies your referral code before purchasing, both you and the buyer receive a +1 pending boost applied to the next souvenir. Referral codes are reusable — you can share them widely. Self-referral is blocked.

🎟 Rule 10 — Promo / Community Codes

The game operator can set community promo codes via environment variables (e.g. DISCORD, XFOLLOW, LAUNCH). These are reusable, give +1 or +2 tier boosts, and are distributed through social channels to reward community participation.

⚙️ Rule 11 — Boost Stacking & Cap

All boosts stack additively, but the effective tier is capped at Tier 4 (maximum: 50% legendary odds). There is no scenario where legendary is guaranteed. A player with multiple boosts active can reach Tier 4 odds even with a short hold time.

🎨 Rule 12 — Souvenir Personality

Souvenir images are AI-generated (fal.ai / FLUX with LoRA fine-tune). Each souvenir has a 25% chance of a happy/smiling expression and a 5% chance of being a female variant — both are entirely random and cut across all rarity tiers. A female legendary souvenir is exceptionally rare (~0.25% of all mints).

4. Hold Duration Tiers — Updated NEW

Tiers have been compressed from a 3-month max to a 30-day max. This change is reflected in both the smart contract (_holdTier()) and the backend (getRarityTier()).

❌ Old Tiers (Too Long)

  • < 24h → Common
  • 1–7 days → Rare
  • 1–4 weeks → Epic
  • 1–3 months → Legendary
  • 3+ months → Max

✅ New Tiers (Compressed)

  • < 6h → Common
  • 6h – 48h → Rare
  • 2–7 days → Epic
  • 7–30 days → Legendary
  • 30+ days → Max

Impact: A player who holds for 10 days now reaches Legendary tier (30% legendary chance). Under the old system they'd only be in Rare territory. This makes the aspiration gap achievable within a single focused week of play — more compatible with crypto attention cycles.

Design tension: The boost cap at Tier 4 (50% legendary) is sound — legendary is never guaranteed. However, a well-equipped player can reach max odds from a 0-hour hold. 7. Overpay Mechanism — Deep Dive

The overpay boost is the most elegant mechanic in the system because it's entirely on-chain, creates a real financial commitment, and is self-regulating. Paying more gets you better souvenir odds but also raises the floor the next buyer has to pay — meaning aggressive overpayers are also pricing themselves harder to sell.

Hidden nuance: A player who pays double the asking price (100%+ overpay, +4 boost) must also set a new asking price ≥ 125% of what they paid. So a 2× overpay creates a 2.5× price floor for the next buyer. This is a powerful self-regulating mechanism that hasn't been prominently communicated to players.

9. Token Economy & Burn Mechanic

The trade-in system creates a deflationary loop within the souvenir economy:

  • Souvenir minted → held in wallet or sold on OpenSea
  • If burned: common/rare souvenirs are sacrificed for a better chance at the next
  • Burn address: 0x000000000000000000000000000000000000dEaD
  • Burned souvenirs are marked "CHARRED" in the gallery and their code is recoverable by the burner's wallet

Potential legendary loop: A player who mints a legendary souvenir could burn it for +4 boost → reach 50% legendary odds again → repeat. This creates a power-user loop that concentrates legendary supply among active burners. Consider capping trade-in from legendary at +3.

Secondary Market (OpenSea)

All souvenirs appear automatically on OpenSea as an ERC-721 collection on Base. The site links directly to each token's OpenSea page with "View" and "Sell on OpenSea" buttons. OpenSea honours the ERC-2981 royalty (5% to creator). Blur and most aggregators do not.

12. Open Questions for Opus

The hold-duration tier system and the boost system are somewhat in tension: a player with stacked boosts can reach 50% legendary odds from a 0-hour hold, which undermines the primary hold-time incentive. Should boosts be capped more aggressively (e.g. max effective Tier 3, not Tier 4) to preserve hold time as the dominant variable?
Should burning a legendary souvenir grant +4 boost (enabling a legendary factory loop) or be capped at +3? What is the right trade-off between rewarding sacrifice and preventing inflation?
Is 50% the right ceiling for legendary odds? Would 35% or 40% preserve more scarcity while still feeling aspirational?
The loyalty system resets after each claim — you must earn new holds to claim again. An alternative: permanent accumulation where each hold permanently adds +0.5 to a capped loyalty multiplier. Which creates better long-term retention?
Rarity probability weights are transparent in the UI (players can see exact percentages). Does full transparency improve or reduce engagement? There's an argument for mystery: "you might get legendary" vs "you have a 1% chance."
Should the female potato variant (5%) be restricted to Rare+ tiers only, making female commons impossible and female legendaries the true unicorn? Or does cross-tier availability create more surprise?
Boost state is in-memory. The three options are: (a) persist to Postgres/Supabase, (b) move boosts fully on-chain, (c) make boosts stateless (derive from on-chain data only). Which architecture is best for a game of this size?
The 5% creator fee is permanent and on-chain. Is 5% the right number for long-term sustainability? Too high disincentivises buyers at scale; too low undersells the creator's value. Most Harberger-style games use 1-3%.
Should there be a hard legendary supply cap (e.g. max 50 legendaries ever minted)? A cap creates scarcity and secondary market value but could also feel arbitrary and creates a cliff where legendary minting stops entirely.
The game currently has no time pressure or deadline. Would a "season" structure (e.g. 90-day rounds, leaderboard resets, a legendary guaranteed at season end) create better engagement cycles? Or does the open-ended nature serve the game better?
There are currently two separate rarity rolls: one on-chain (hold + overpay only) and one off-chain (all boosts). The displayed rarity comes from the IPFS metadata (off-chain roll). Is this acceptable complexity, or should the system converge to a single authoritative source of truth?
